To Ask or Ask Not

July 26, 2015

In my entire time spent in DBC's Phase 0, I never posted a question on the Google+ Community, never attended office hours, and never reached out for help with a technical issue in any capacity. Some might say this was stubborn of me, others might say asking questions could have saved me time/effort. It wasn't like I never had problems or questions about the work, I am still very much a novice in terms of coding. And it also wasn't that I was refusing to ask questions to keep some sort of reputation or pride. What actually happened was that I came in to Phase 0 with the knowledge of how to get my questions answered efficiently.

One great thing about being a novice is that most of your questions have been asked before. Everyone who was ever a novice at JavaScript, I imagine, found themselves confused by the different ways to define and declare a function. Luckily, there are wonderful resources to answer such questions. I made great use of Google during Phase 0, and most of the time, Google pointed me to Stack Overflow. Such resources, where you glean the answers you're looking for from other people's questions and examples, must be used lightly though. I found that if I had to spend too much time with someone else's issue, I was probably overstretching to apply it to my own situation and therefor wasn't going to find much help with it.

Another strategy I learned through all my Googles are that very specific questions tend to yield better results. Today, for example, I paired with someone on a challenge where we were supposed to make a function that accepts integers as arguments and returns the English words that represent that int. When we got stuck, my pair's first Google was something like: "Change integers to words Ruby example". Needless to say, he found an example that didn't quite match what we were trying to do and promptly went down a rabbit hole trying to apply this solution to our own code. The strategy that I've found works best is to follow your own path and ask more specific questions along the way. I Googled things like "Ruby change string to int" and "Ruby delete char from string".

Now that Phase 1 is starting, I do plan on asking my peers and teachers questions from time to time, but I plan on using the same method I used online in Phase 0. The answers may be available from more than one resource, but simple questions always seem to return the clearest solutions.
